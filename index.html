Games metaphors play

07-12-16

Let’s start by banging some quotes together:

I can think of no better expression to characterize these similarities than “family resemblances”; for the various resemblances between members of a family: build, features, colour of eyes, gait, temperament, etc. etc. overlap and criss-cross in the same way. – And I shall say: “games” form a family.

Ludwig WittgensteinFamily Resemblances WikiPedia

Writing free verse is like playing tennis with the net down.

Robert FrostRobert Frost Wikipedia

I’ve a hunch that what makes games and metaphors ‘work’ is related. In the first quote Wittgenstein is trying to solve the ‘problem’ of defining what it is we’re talking abbout when we talk about games. We have this experience of feeling that although almost anything might be a game there are definitely things that just aren’t games: rain, global warming, gardening and cosmetics picking things at random that just aren’t games. By contrast Cricket, Mornington Crescent, Quidditch and what a cat does with a mouse rather than eat it are games. I see that what holds these togther as games is as Wittgenstein says that they “…overlap and criss-cross”.

Now that other quote - the tennis match with the net down. I take from this that Robert Frost did not entirely approve of free verse, but what interests me is the picture of a game with something missing, and metaphors like games have a family resemblance one to others but also there is a family resemblance to other metaphors that work for me. Its why I think that metaphors that are doing their job properly feel complete and also like games that they mean something in the same way as games mean something.

Some people think football is a matter of life and death. I assure you, it’s much more serious than that.

Bill ShanklyBill Shankly WikiPedia

For those for whom football means something, it is both a whole course of study near endless topic of conversation and source of meaning. So it is with the metaphors in any life. To an outsider they are like football to the non-fan - just so many grown men hoofing a bladder around a park for 90 minutes in the rain. Or if you prefer as Mitchell and Web have it:

Curiously now I find myself thinking that I’ve kept my own metaphorical cards close to my chest - the family of metaphors that give me mean are. That’s a post for another time.

Persons into metaphors - don't go

07-12-16

Looking no further than recent history there are many examples of where metaphoring people leads to inhumanity. Referring to particular ethnic groups as rats or cochroaches or immigrants as foul liquids these formulations are the sign that ill treatment of any group so labelled is forseeable if not imminent.
The reasons are obvious - people find hurting other people prohibited - morally / religiously and physically difficult for all but the pathological and taking away the humanity of a group is therefore the mechanism to enable the eroding of that prohibition. Its why for example staying on your feet is essential in any kind of brawl, once your down, you’ll find your humanity overlooked.
If we extend ourselves from metaphor to other kinds of figuration for example synecdoche (the replacement of the part for the whole) referring to workers as ‘hands’- still used in the fashion industry or ‘boots on the ground’ for soliders it seems that a similar but not the same logic is at work, but these forms aren’t derogatory. They may be unwelcome but they are meant to express a range of feelings. Politicians and sportsmen in leadership roles are often described as “a safe pair of hands”. We talkabout a person being the “main man” or “right hand man” What about the singer described as “the voice” or the ‘super model’ who was called “the body” clearly these are not meant perjoratively.

In all of these cases - even in objectional and sexist examples - you can provide your own examples - where the parts are still identifiably human or even a thing that retains the imprint of a human is worthy of a human response - Van Gogh’s painting of shoes or the famous empty Dicken’s chair etching:

Humans can see humanness in almost anything - but when we’re able to label something as not human this seems to license us to access our inhumanity.
